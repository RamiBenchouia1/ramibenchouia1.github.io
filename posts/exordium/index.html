Exordium

A Financial Times article1 from June 14, 2021 titled “Algeria on the brink as pandemic and low oil price take their toll” caught my eye the other day. As I read on, I was struck by a sense of disquietude both at the implications of the article and at my benighted state regarding the history and trajectory of the country. And from thence came the impetus for the discourse that follows. The text that follows is my attempt to gain an understanding of the issue, its causes, and potential future directions, and to communicate it as simply as possible to anyone interested in the topic, which need not only be Algerians, or economists, or even political scientists, but anyone interested in political economy and neoliberalism.

I imagine that it is not entirely uncommon to find a Western financialnews publication hinting at the neoliberalization of a wavering country’s economy, and indeed, this seems to be the case in the aforementioned FT Article. To be clear, the article takes no explicit stance on the ‘best’ way to go forward for the country and seems to be of a more expository ilk rather than a persuasive one. The article primarily, and quite succinctly, describes and etymologizes the current state of the country’s economy, citing some key contributing factors that begot the country’s current economic malaise and prognosticating its trajectory. this cursory account the article mainly focuses on the military governments reluctance to “introduce reforms that would unshackle the private sector, incentivize investments and bring transparency to an economic system built on a web of vested interests and clientelism fueled by petrodollars”, points out Algeria’s rampant clientelism and “crony capitalism”, although given its cursory nature, I don’t think it would be reasonable to expect a nuanced account or suggestions or a reasonable weighing of the options available.

While I don’t take a stance on whether or not neoliberalization is the best option, I do however think that the article trivializes the situation and does not do much in the way of offering a nuanced or realistic approach to solving Algeria’s economic woes. But before that discussion can be had, one must first acquaint oneself with the arduous task of demarcating these woes.
