What’s in a word?2 You’ll certainly get a different answer depending on whom you ask. One point that experts and amateurs can all appreciate though is the fact that the words we utter on a daily basis have entire histories behind them; they are imbued with stories of their creation, their itineration, and their evolution. This is especially true of the jargon we encounter in the scientific and medical world. In medicine, the linguaphile finds a veritable treasure trove of words as the language of medicine is one replete with metaphor and mystery. The daunting3 polysyllabic4 words serve “as a shorthand for an elaborate tale of disease”5. Well, if there is any truth to the saying that “an enemy is someone whose you don’t know”, perhaps exploring the story of some of these intimidating words can help us become friends. Today, we’ll start with the word: syringomyelia.

Etymologically it is derived from the Greek prefix “syrinx/syring–” meaning fistula/tube/channel6 (think: syringe, which has the same etymological origin) and the modern latin suffix “–myel” which hails from the greek “–muelos” meaning marrow and is often used in reference to the spinal cord7. Put the two together and you get a word that means tube in the marrow. More generally, a syrinx is a fluid-filled cyst located in the nervous system, and usually pops up in 2 main areas: the spinal cord (which would be called a syringomyelia) or the brain stem (which would be called a syringobulbia).

According to Merriam-webster, the first known use of syringomyelia was in 1880.8 However, a 2012 paper by Olivier Walusinksi that was published in the journal Vesalius seems to indicate that the term was actually coined in the late 1830s by Charles-Prosper Ollivier d’Angers.9

10

Charles has a bit of an interesting back story. Unsure of what exactly he wanted to pursue as his career, he enrolled in the French Military School at 17 and became an exceptional officer in Napoleon’s Young Guard and even fought in the battle of waterloo. Now if it weren’t for the unfortunate fate of the French in that battle, we may never have had the word syringomyelia. The French loss allowed the royalists to reclaim power, ultimately leading to Charles seeing his way out of the armed forces and turning to the medical sciences. At the ripe young age of 27 he penned his medical thesis, entitled “Essai sur l’anatomie et les vices de conformation de la moelle epiniere chez l’homme” (or, “essay on the anatomy and confirmation defects of the spinal cord in man”). What is truly astonishing is that, without the use of a microscope, he was able to formulate the anatomy, physiology, and pathology of the spinal cord. His magnum opus “De la moelle epiniere et de ses maladies” (“On the spinal cord and its diseases”) was published just 1 year after his medical thesis and it was in the third edition/publication of this work in 1837 that we find the introduction of the term “syringomyelia” along with the earliest known and most accurate description of this specific disorder.

The initial symptoms include pain, numbness of the hands due to sensory loss, stiffness of the legs, scoliosis, vertigo, dysphonia (difficulty in speaking), and cough-induced pain.11,12 The term became more commonly known when a clinical depiction of syringomyelia was first described in Germany by Otto von Kahler and Friedrich Schultze in 188213 and a subsequent outline of the disease pathology in relation to the Chiari Malformation14 by John Cleland in 1883.15 These developments ultimately led to Abbe and Coley performing a myelotomy to drain the syrinx cavities of the spinal cord, thusly becoming the first to complete a neurological procedure to treat syringomyelia16
