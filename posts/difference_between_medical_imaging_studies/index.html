Intro

I was looking at something the other day that referenced a few different kinds of high-tech medical imaging technologies, and got to thinking about how there are quite a few different kinds of imaging machinery and technologies used these days, and it wasn’t entirely clear to me what the difference is between them and in what sorts of situations they are used in medicine. So I wanted to clarify that for myself, and try to produce an easy guide for others who might have had the same question.

CAT/CT Scans

I’ll start with CAT scans (also known as CT scans). The CAT in ‘CAT scan’ stands for Computed Axial Tomography, and CT is just a shortening of this acronym (i.e., CT = Computed Tomography). CAT scan was technically the first of the two terms to be used, but CT scan has become increasingly popular and is likely what you’ll hear or see being referenced1. Now, it might be useful to go into what that last letter stands for to get a better understanding of what’s going on in this type of scan.

‘Tomography’ is a word that seems to have popped up in the 1930’s, and etymologically is derived from the Greek tomos meaning ‘slice’ or ‘section’2 and the greek suffix -graphy meaning ‘a process or form of drawing, writing’3. So, when you put these two components together, we see that tomography is simply a method of measuring an entity (in most cases an organ, bone, or other such body part) by taking multiple slice snapshots of it and compiling them together. This tomographical method of imaging is one that is common to various medical imaging technologies (including MRI and PET scans). However, when a medical professional refers to a CT or CAT scan, they are likely referring to a donut-shaped machine which the body is placed in and which contains a mechanism of emitting a series of narrow beams of ionizing radiation (usually in the form of X-rays) through the region of interest as the beam-emitting module moves through the machine in a 360 degree arc.

Sometimes, medical professionals will administer a contrast dye into your blood stream so as to aid in adding definition to the resulting image and allowing the radiologists and physicians who will end up viewing the image to have a more clear and distinct picture.

In a medical setting, CT scans can be used to image anything from soft tissue structures such as blood vessels and organs (lungs, brain, etc), to imaging osseous structures (i.e. bones). It can help with the diagnosis and identification of blood clots, bone fractures (including more subtle fractures not typically seen on X-ray imaging), as well as injuries and abnormal growths in the soft tissue and viscera. Interestingly enough, MRI scans also do a pretty good job of imaging soft tissue, so one might be led to wonder what would cause a physician to prefer ordering a CT over an MRI. While MRIs typically provide a more nuanced image of the soft-tissue, they are quite costly, often a bit more of a time-intensive process, and, for some patients, a bit too claustrophobic. CT scans are more adept at capturing the bony structures of the body, take less time than an MRI, and aren’t nearly as costly (not that they don’t cost a pretty penny themselves).

CTA (CT Angiography)

Some may have heard of a specific kind of CT scan called a CT Angiography (abbreviated CTA). This is a CT scan geared towards imaging the body’s blood vessels and involves the administration of a contrast dye, usually iodine- or gadolinium-based. These types of imaging studies are especially useful when identifying or diagnosing aneurysms as well as blockages in the body’s vasculature.

X-rays

X-ray imaging is perhaps the most basic of the imaging studies, and definitely the earliest one on the scene. In 1895 Wilhelm Rontgen laid the foundations for modern day radiology with the invention of the X-ray. The best way to think of how an X-ray works is to think of the machine as shining a special kind of light on the body part of interest, and the shadow that is cast on a special film behind the body part is the X-ray image (also called an X-ray radiograph). In the old days, the receiving film was photographic film, but these days there are many other types of detectors used to produce digital images.
