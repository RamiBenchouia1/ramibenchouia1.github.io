Exordium

A Financial Times article1 from June 14, 2021 titled “Algeria on the brink as pandemic and low oil price take their toll” caught my eye the other day. As I read on, I was struck by a sense of disquietude both at the implications of the article and at my benighted state regarding the history and trajectory of the country. And thus came the impetus for the text that follows. I have attempted to gain an understanding of the issue, its causes, and potential future directions, and to communicate this information as simply as possible to anyone interested in the topic, which need not only be Algerians, economists, political scientists, or the like, but anyone interested in political economy and neoliberalism.

I imagine that it is not entirely uncommon2 to find a Western financial news publication hinting at the neoliberalization of a wavering country’s economy, and indeed, this seems to be the case in the aforementioned FT Article. To be clear, the article takes no explicit stance on the ‘best’ way to go forward for the country and seems to be of a more expository ilk rather than a persuasive one. The article primarily, and quite succinctly3, describes the current state of the country’s economy – with a general/vague account of the contributing factors that begot the country’s current economic malaise – and, overall, gives the impression that Algeria’s prognosis is quite poor, in every sense of the word. The article mainly focuses on the government’s reluctance to “introduce reforms that would unshackle the private sector, incentivize investments and bring transparency to an economic system built on a web of vested interests and clientelism fueled by petrodollars”, and highlights Algeria’s rampant clientelism and “crony capitalism”.

Given the articles perfunctory nature, one would be remiss to expect from it any semblance of a nuanced or detailed account of how Algeria got to the position it is in, or what realistic solutions it has to preempt its impending economic demise. It is my hope that the text that follows has retained a semblance of fidelity to my aim to reconcile these criticisms of the article. And so, as the King of Hearts advises in Carroll’s Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland, let us “begin at the beginning”.

The Sick Man of Africa

For Algeria, as for so many other countries in North Africa and the Near East (and the Global South as a whole), the problem seems to lie primarily in two domains: political entrenchment and economic mismanagement (which all too often seem to go hand-in-hand).

It is difficult to pinpoint a single event that is the root cause of some later event in life’s anfractuous reticulum of causality, but for the purpose of practicality, it’s reasonable to start off in 1962 for the matter at hand. This was the year Algeria and the FLN, the National Liberation Front (or in French, le Front de Libération Nationale) –– the politico-military party that spearheaded the independence struggle –– gained independence from its French imperial colonizers. The party quickly bureaucratized and consolidated its competing factions under Houari Boumediene in 1965.

Boumediene implemented thoroughly socialist policies which elevated many out of poverty and created a bourgeoning public sector4. All the while, Algeria remained a one-party state and as time went on, the various faculties of the state (military, government, economy, etc) became deeply interwoven. This set Algeria up to become what is today essentially a praetorian state. And therein lies Algeria’s original sin. The military and armed forces of Algeria are inextricably linked to governance. The power vacuum left in the wake of Boumediene’s untimely death in 1978 and the oil price collapse in the 1980s, rather than ushering in or prompting democratic and liberal reforms5, instead bolstered military power and social discord.

Despite its predilection for military control, it would be inaccurate to claim that Algeria never experimented with democracy. The first open and competitive elections that Algerian’s saw came in 1991. Parliamentary elections were held on December 26, 1991; but before all 430 seats to the People’s National Assembly were decided, a military coup6 brought election proceedings to a halt in January 1992 as the results suggested the Islamist Party (the Islamic Salvation Front, abbreviated FIS for its French acronymical origins of Front Islamique du Salut) was poised to defeat the ruling FLN party7 (which was not in much of a sharing mood given its position of power for the past 30 years, democracy be damned) and would take the spoils of Algeria’s first foray into a democratic duel. What followed was a “dark decade” marred by a sanguinary civil war between various Islamist groups and the Algerian government which extinguished countless thousands of Algerian lives and any hopes for democracy in Algeria.
